From the Editors

Why a bilingual journal

Mathematics is made by people, in places, in languages. We wanted a room where teaching and thinking could happen in Persian and English at once — not translated past one another, but side by side. People and Mathematics is monthly, and it belongs to its readers.

We are interested in the human face of the subject: the teacher who begins with a question, the child whose objection is wiser than the rule, the manuscript that travelled, the room where chalk meets tea. Write to us.

— The Editors

ریاضیات را آدم‌ها می‌سازند؛ در مکان‌ها، در زبان‌ها. می‌خواستیم اتاقی باشد که آموزش و اندیشیدن، هم‌زمان به فارسی و انگلیسی در آن رخ دهد — نه ترجمه‌ای از کنارِ هم، بلکه در کنارِ هم. «آدم‌ها و ریاضیات» ماهانه است و از آنِ خوانندگانش. برای ما بنویسید.

— سردبیران

The Long Conversation

A teacher who has spent a working life at the blackboard on what it means to learn mathematics with, and not merely from, other people.

Interview by the Editors    Photographs from Shemiran    18 min read
photo — teacher at the blackboard, Shemiran (replace with real image)

He arrives early, before the students, and writes a single question on the board. Not the topic, not the date — a question. By the time the room fills, the question has already begun to do its work, and the lesson is less a delivery than a conversation that everyone has half-joined before sitting down.

“I never wanted to be the person who knows,” he says. “I wanted to be the person who is still curious in front of them. The moment a teacher stops being curious, the mathematics goes flat — it becomes a set of moves to memorise rather than a thing to wonder about.”

We talked for an afternoon about the students who argued with him, the ones who were right to, and the slow patience of letting a wrong idea be spoken aloud so that it could be examined rather than buried. He keeps a notebook of objections — sentences students have said that, in his words, “were smarter than the rule they were breaking.”

Mathematics, in his telling, is something people do together in a room: a culture before it is a curriculum. The proof on the board matters, but so does the cup of tea afterward, the corridor argument, the letter that arrives months later from a student who finally understood.

The Word That Became Algebra

How al-jabr — “the restoring” — travelled from a Baghdad manuscript into every classroom on earth.

The Editors    9 min read
manuscript page — al-Khwārizmī

A word can carry a whole way of thinking across a thousand years. When a ninth-century scholar in Baghdad wrote of al-jabr — the restoring or completing of broken quantities — he was naming a method, not founding a brand. Yet the word outlived the method’s every particular and became the name of a discipline.

The journey is the point: from manuscript to translation to textbook, each generation receiving the idea slightly changed and passing it on. To teach algebra is to stand at one end of that long relay and hand the baton forward.

We trace the word through the rooms it passed: the House of Wisdom, the workshops of translators, the first European printers who set it in type, the schoolbooks that fixed its modern shape. At each step, people — named and unnamed — chose to carry it.

وقتی منفی در منفی نادرست می‌نمود

اعتراضِ سرسختانه‌ی یک کودک، و آنچه درباره‌ی جدی‌گرفتنِ برداشت‌ها به ما می‌آموزد.

امیر اصغری    ۷ دقیقه خواندن
photo — number line in chalk

کودکی در ردیف آخر دستش را بالا می‌برد و می‌گوید این درست نیست. منفی در منفی چطور می‌تواند مثبت شود؟ او حق دارد که بپرسد؛ و وظیفه‌ی ما این نیست که اعتراضش را خاموش کنیم، بلکه این است که آن را جدی بگیریم.

این متنِ جای‌گزین است. آن را با مقاله‌ی نهایی جایگزین کنید. ساختار دو ستونی و راست‌چین، شکلِ خود را برای هر اندازه از متن حفظ می‌کند.

برداشت‌های نادرست، پنجره‌هایی به فهم‌اند. وقتی به‌جای پاک‌کردنِ آن‌ها، به آن‌ها گوش می‌دهیم، می‌بینیم که اغلب منطقی درونی دارند — منطقی که اگر دنبالش کنیم، به ریاضیاتِ درست‌تر می‌رسیم.

گفت‌وگوی کلاس، نه برای رسیدن به پاسخِ سریع، بلکه برای آشکارکردنِ همین منطق‌هاست. این بخش را آزادانه ویرایش کنید.
